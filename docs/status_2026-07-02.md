# Score-Bundle Models — status, 2026-07-02

*Plain-language summary for review. The technical details, exact commands, and full
tables live in the repository (`docs/phase1_calibration_results.md`,
`docs/downstream_tasks_results.md`, `docs/formulation.tex`).*

**One-paragraph recap of the project.** We are building a probabilistic model that,
given the printed score and a recording of a performance (as piano MIDI for now),
recovers *how* each written note was actually played — its timing, its length
(articulation), and its loudness (dynamics) — together with an honest statement of how
confident the model is about each number. The key idea is a "score graph": notes that
are close in the score should be played in related ways, and the model uses that
structure both to fill in notes it cannot see well and to keep its confidence estimates
honest. A small music language model (a neural network we trained ourselves on the
MAESTRO piano corpus) supplies a first guess per note; the graph models what the guess
misses.

### 2. A correction to the headline result — found, fixed, and re-measured

While re-verifying the January pipeline we found a subtle flaw: the neural network's
per-note summary was computed at a position where it could *see the note's own loudness
value* — the very thing it was later asked to predict. Its apparently excellent loudness
predictions were partly it "copying its own input" rather than genuine prediction. We
fixed this by recomputing the summaries from the score alone (every note fed a neutral
placeholder loudness).

We also fixed a known numerical fragility: on a minority of evaluation runs the
automatic tuning of the model's noise level collapsed to zero, making the model
absurdly overconfident. A simple guard (the noise level may not drop below 5% of the
observed variability) fixes it. Both fixes were measured separately, one change at a
time.

**The corrected headline still supports the central claim, and now with statistical
significance.** On 30 held-out test pieces (never seen by the language model — we also
filter out any test performance whose twin appears in the language model's training
data) with 40% of notes hidden:

- The full model (language-model guess + graph) recovers hidden notes with the lowest
  error of all methods (RMSE 0.394, 95% CI [0.356, 0.417]) *and* the best-calibrated
  uncertainty (its "90% confidence" intervals contain the truth 92.2% of the time;
  log-score −0.314, the best of all methods).
- Paired comparisons across pieces show the graph's contribution is statistically
  significant on *both* accuracy and calibration (it beats the same guess without the
  graph on every axis), and the language-model guess adds a small but significant
  accuracy gain over using no guess at all.
- Honestly stated: the language model's advantage is *smaller* than the January numbers
  suggested — much of its apparent advantage on loudness was the flaw above.

### 3. Three downstream demonstrations that the graph structure is useful

Chosen for having objective success measures and honest baselines; all run on the same
held-out, contamination-filtered test pieces.

**(a) Error spotting (anomaly detection).** We deliberately corrupt 5% of notes (e.g. a
note played much too loud or held much too long) and ask each model to rank all notes
by suspiciousness. Ranking quality is measured by a standard score (AUROC; 0.5 = random
guessing, 1.0 = perfect). The graph model reaches **0.984** vs **0.943** for the
best structure-free baseline — and the gain holds on every one of timing, articulation,
and loudness. When the injected errors are made subtler (harder to spot), the graph's
advantage *grows* (0.85 → 0.94), because judging a note against its neighbours is exactly
what lets you notice one that is only slightly off for its local context. This task
rewards exactly what the thesis claims: trustworthy per-note uncertainty. **Verdict:
clear win.**

**(b) Cleaning up noisy transcriptions (denoising).** Automatic transcriptions of piano
recordings (the route to very large training corpora) are noisy. We simulate this by
adding noise to every note and asking models to recover the true values. When the noise
level is known, the graph model recovers the truth with clearly lower error than
per-note statistical shrinkage (the standard alternative) at equally honest confidence
(both ~90% coverage) — better on both axes, at every noise level tested. *Honest
negative:* estimating the noise level from a single piece is unreliable (the model
becomes overconfident); applications should calibrate the noise level per transcription
system, which is realistic, or estimate it across many pieces (future work).

**(c) Completing a performance from an excerpt (rendering).** Hear the performer play
only the opening (10–50% of the piece), predict the expression of everything after —
a much harder "extrapolation" task than the random-gaps benchmark. Here the two
ingredients separate cleanly: the language-model guess is what makes extrapolation work
(it generalizes across the whole piece where the hand-built baselines cannot), while the
graph structure helps only when the hidden notes have *nearby heard notes* to borrow
from — i.e. on filling scattered gaps, not on predicting a whole unheard second half.
**Verdict: partial** — use the language-model guess for the global shape and add the
graph where observed notes are close by.

### 4. Two small, tested robustness upgrades (each measured one at a time)

- **Noise-level floor** (the fix above): turns the occasional overconfidence collapse
  into a non-event; on the headline it moves the full model's log-score from a spiky
  +0.08 (heavy-tailed across runs) to a clean −0.314, and makes the graph's advantage
  statistically significant.
- **Per-channel confidence rescaling** (fit on training pieces only): brings the "90%
  confidence" intervals from 92.2% down to 90.0% coverage exactly and lowers the
  calibration error, at a small log-score cost. Offered as an optional final step for
  when exact coverage is the requirement.

### 5. A finding that changes the scaling story

Re-running the "does a bigger language model give a better prior?" comparison under the
corrected (leak-free) setup: **the bigger model (1.3×, lower perplexity) gives an
essentially identical downstream result** (RMSE 0.394 vs 0.394). The apparent scaling
benefit in the January numbers was the same loudness leak — a lower-perplexity model
reconstructs its own loudness input more faithfully, which masqueraded as a better prior.
Practical implication: judge language-model quality by the *downstream* task, not
perplexity, before investing in scaling up on large (noisy, transcribed) corpora.
